Metrics & Severity Glossary

Understanding Severity: 0 dBFS is digital maximum (clipping). -120 dBFS is digital silence. Because measurement mic hardware gains vary, these severity badges assume a standard consumer microphone curve. A reading of "Extreme" indicates signal levels approaching the hardware limits.
Low (< -75 dBFS) Normal (-75 to -55 dBFS) High (-55 to -35 dBFS) Extreme (> -35 dBFS)

Vibration Proxy (< 20 Hz)

Also known as Infrasound. Felt physically rather than heard. Proxy for structure-borne energy from traffic, industrial machinery, or heavy HVAC compressors.

LFN (20 - 100 Hz)

Deep bass frequencies that easily pass through solid walls and windows. Associated with the annoying "rumble" of diesel engines, idling trucks, or subwoofer bass.

LF (100 - 250 Hz)

Upper bass to lower mid-range spectrum. Contains the "boominess" of a poorly treated acoustic room, and fundamental frequencies of male speech and standard appliances.

Mid-Frequency (250 - 2kHz)

The "Speech Band" or general background noise. Crucial for context: If LFN is High, but MF is also High, the environment is just generally loud. If LFN is High but MF is Low, the LFN is "unmasked" and highly perceptible/annoying.

Broadband (10 - 20kHz)

The total overall acoustic energy detected across the entire audible spectrum. Useful as a general baseline room-noise volume.

Peak Resonance

The specific, single frequency experiencing the absolute highest acoustic energy in real-time. Tracking a steady peak (e.g., a locked 60Hz line) helps pinpoint specific electrical hums or motor RPMs.

Hardware Notes: Standard device microphones artificially roll off sub-bass frequencies. For accurate LFN and air/structure-borne vibration measurements, select a calibrated USB measurement microphone (e.g., miniDSP UMIK-1) from the dropdown once connected.
